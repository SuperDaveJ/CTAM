Shift the Focus

Shifting focus is another good strategy for stimulating creative thought. Take a look at some ways that you can shift your focus. Then we’ll try some examples.

Humor

Jokes often shift the focus of information. The punch line offers a completely new perspective that makes you think differently than what you had thought all along.

Deliberate Provocation

De Bono suggests shifting focus by creating deliberate provocations that are so strange that you just can’t accept them. This provokes your mind into reconsidering the generally accepted.

Thinking Backwards

Richards J. Heuer recommends training yourself to think backwards.

Consider an event that you think is unlikely to happen, and put yourself into a future where it has happened. Then think backwards about what must have happened in the past to have set the state for the event. What actions did people take in preparation for the event?

Thinking this way can help open new avenues for current research and may identify current gaps in your intelligence that need to be filled. This can be particularly helpful for events that have a low probability but very serious consequences should they occur. Not being able to imagine something makes people think it is less likely to occur. Therefore, it is important to imagine as many scenarios as
possible—even unlikely ones.

Crystal Ball

Richards J. Heuer recommends the Crystal Ball method to get a unique perspective on an analytic line of reasoning and to force yourself to think about a side track that you might have missed in your initial analysis.

Imagine that a perfect intelligence source (a “crystal ball) has told you that a certain assumption is wrong.  Now develop a scenario to explain how the crystal ball could be right. If you can do this, it would suggest that your assumption is open to question.
