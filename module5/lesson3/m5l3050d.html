The Process for Generating Hypotheses

The process for generating hypotheses consists of four steps. Let’s see how we can apply that process to an example. Suppose you learned that the officer in charge at the border has observed that more lower-level criminal offenders are arrested at the border than higher-level offenders. Let’s generate a hypothesis about why that is happening.

Step 1. Identify the major problem.

First, you need to clearly state the problem you’re trying to solve. In the intelligence world, this is called framing the question, which you learned about earlier.

As you think more deeply about the issue of the border arrests, you ask specific questions to put parameters around what you are being asked to analyze.

In this case, you have an observation about the numbers of arrests at the border. There certainly seems to be a pattern to these arrests. You are being asked to determine what that pattern might mean.

Step 2. Make an educated guess

Second, you review the data and make an educated guess about what you think is going on. At this point, you may actually have several guesses.

For example, the observation about border arrests could mean that:

  • Higher-level criminals are better at not getting caught

  • There are more lower-level criminals crossing the border than higher-level criminals.

  • Border agents are targeting lower-level criminals.

  • Higher-level criminals are not participating in crimes across the border.

Step 3. Identify the major variables

In our example, some of the variables are:

  • Number of higher-level criminals arrested at the border

  • Number of lower-level criminals arrested at the border

  • Number of arrests in general at the border

  • The border agent’s observations

Step 4. Write a simple, declarative statement
Finally, write a simple, declarative statement. Do not use the words “I think” at the beginning of the sentence.

For the border arrest example, you’d need to review the available data to determine which of the hypotheses you generated is most likely. Suppose, for example, that the data you analyzed pointed strongly to a conclusion that higher-level criminals are not participating in crimes across the border. That is your hypothesis.
