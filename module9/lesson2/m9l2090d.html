A Past Event Influencing its Present Outcome

There is every probability that he would have been comfortably re-elected in November 1964. His approval rating in autumn 1963 was almost 60 percent, the economy was thriving and Vietnam caused only minor anxiety.

A Democrat, rather than Richard Nixon, would probably have won in 1968. Thus no Watergate, and no President Jimmy Carter. Ronald Reagan might have come to power, but as Sean Wilentz argued in the New York Times, there might have been no Republican "Southern Strategy" along the lines conceived by Nixon, which has produced the most important shift in the U.S. electoral map of the past half-century.

The Bush family might not have replaced the Kennedys as America's most successful political dynasty. Both have especially loyal political advisors: in JFK's case his brother Robert; For George W. Bush his political strategist Karl Rover and his Vice-President, Dick Cheney. And now, as then, their supporters may have urged American into controversial wars.

In summary, the mind handles information through perception and memory processes, both of which have associated cognitive errors. Both can be influenced by the lens of mindsets. This explains the variety of perspectives that result from the same information.
